# Motivation

I first heard about Polymarket and Kalshi when I realized that certain events, before they were officially announced, could already be predicted on these platforms, sometimes through insider trading. For a while, I used Polymarket purely as an information source to gauge whether something was likely to happen, rather than waiting for the news to catch up. I wasn't trading at all during that period.

Then I stumbled onto the BTC Up/Down markets, 5-minute and 15-minute windows where you simply predict whether BTC will finish above or below a reference price. It seemed straightforward, almost too easy. My first reaction was that it was just gambling. That changed when I watched [this video from the "Art of Problem" YouTube channel about prediction markets](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ngX1nIvnMOM). It helped me understand the mechanics behind them, what drives people to participate, how prices reflect collective belief, and how outcomes are settled. The core idea clicked: if more people put money behind outcome X, the market price moves toward X, and that price becomes a real-time probability signal.

What drew me in further was the technology stack behind Polymarket. It uses blockchain for settlement, an off-chain order book for fast matching, and Chainlink price feeds for trustless resolution on short-duration markets like BTC Up/Down. I spent time understanding how it all works at the smart contract level, how USDC collateral gets locked, how conditional tokens are minted and burned, and how the whole system stays honest through arbitrage. That deep dive was genuinely fun.

Eventually I decided to start trading. I went in knowing that these markets can be unpredictable. No amount of RSI, MACD, or EMA indicators will give you a reliable edge in predicting whether BTC goes up or down over five minutes. There will always be rounds where you lose. But that's fine. The key insight is that you don't need to win every time. If you can get 4 out of 5 right and have a solid exit strategy for the ones that go wrong (stop losses, emergency sells), you can keep your losses small and let the wins add up.

I started building a bot around this idea. My first strategy was called `late-entry`. The concept was to wait, identify which markets looked favorable, and then enter with a position to hold until resolution. Over time I realized that holding until the end isn't the only way to profit. You can also exit early by selling into strength, the classic buy low, sell high approach. What makes these 5-minute markets interesting is that certain patterns tend to appear during specific time windows of the day. I spent many hours watching the live order book (using the `scripts/orderbook.ts` script) to spot these patterns.

The original engine wasn't built for this kind of flexibility, so I rewrote it from scratch. The new design gives each market its own lifecycle that runs independently. The engine itself is just an orchestrator. It launches market lifecycles, and each lifecycle runs the strategy internally. I named it `early-bird` because it always looks ahead to upcoming markets rather than jumping into the current one. Once this architecture was in place, I took the old `late-entry` logic and turned it into a strategy that runs inside the new engine. You can find a simplified version of it in the `engine/strategy` folder as a simulation strategy. It's not the actual strategy I trade with, but it shows what the engine is capable of.

This repository is the public-facing version of the engine. It contains the exact same engine code I use daily. The only difference is that the real strategy implementations are kept private. It syncs regularly from my private repo. The engine also includes a simulation environment that mirrors real Polymarket behavior: real order book data, simulated network delays, partial fills, and failure cases. Building a simulation that behaves identically to production was one of my main goals, because I wanted to test strategies with full confidence before putting real money on the line.

There are other open source trading engines out there, but most of them feel like black boxes and I'm never sure whether the person who published it actually uses it themselves. My approach is different. I use this engine every day, and my goal in making it public is for others to learn from what I've built. If you're interested in getting started, head over to [GUIDE.md](GUIDE.md).
